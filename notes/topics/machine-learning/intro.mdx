Machine learning is the art of writing programs that improve at a task by looking
at examples rather than by following hand-coded rules. Instead of telling the
computer *how* to recognize a cat, you show it thousands of labelled pictures and
let it tune millions of internal parameters until its guesses line up with reality.

<Callout type="info" title="The one-sentence definition">
A model is a function with adjustable knobs (**parameters**); *learning* is the
process of nudging those knobs to minimize a **loss** — a number that measures how
wrong the model currently is.
</Callout>

## The three paradigms

Almost everything in the field falls into one of three buckets, distinguished by
*what feedback the learner receives*.

<CardGrid columns={3}>
  <Card title="Supervised" icon="🏷️">
    Learn from **labelled** examples — each input comes with the correct answer.
    Used for spam detection, price prediction, and image classification.
    Split into *regression* (predict a number) and *classification* (predict a category).
  </Card>
  <Card title="Unsupervised" icon="🧩">
    Find structure in **unlabelled** data — no answer key. Think clustering
    customers into segments, compressing data, or learning embeddings.
    The model discovers patterns rather than reproducing given targets.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Reinforcement" icon="🎮">
    Learn by **acting** in an environment and collecting **rewards**. An agent
    tries actions, sees consequences, and refines a *policy* over time.
    Powers game-playing agents, robotics, and RLHF for tuning LLMs.
  </Card>
</CardGrid>

## What training actually looks like

During training we repeatedly pass the data through the model (each full pass is an
**epoch**) and watch the loss fall. The catch: we track *two* losses. **Training
loss** measures fit on data the model has seen; **validation loss** measures fit on
held-out data it hasn't. As long as both keep dropping together, learning is healthy.

<Callout type="warning" title="Overfitting: the cardinal sin">
When training loss keeps dropping but **validation loss turns upward**, the model has
stopped *learning* and started *memorizing* — it's fitting the noise in your training
set instead of the underlying signal. It will look brilliant on data it has seen and
fail on anything new. Common cures: gather more data, add **regularization** (dropout,
weight decay), simplify the model, or stop training early at the validation minimum
(here, around **epoch 16**).
</Callout>

## A short history

Machine learning is older than most people assume. A few load-bearing moments:

<Timeline>
  <TimelineItem title="The Perceptron" date="1958" icon="🧠">
    Frank Rosenblatt builds the first trainable artificial neuron. It learns linearly
    separable patterns — and triggers both wild hype and, later, a painful "AI winter"
    when its limits (it can't even learn XOR) become clear.
  </TimelineItem>
  <TimelineItem title="Backpropagation" date="1986" icon="🔁">
    Rumelhart, Hinton & Williams popularize an efficient way to compute gradients
    through *many* layers. Suddenly multi-layer networks are trainable, reviving the field.
  </TimelineItem>
  <TimelineItem title="AlexNet & ImageNet" date="2012" icon="🖼️">
    A deep convolutional network slashes the ImageNet error rate by ~10 points, trained
    on GPUs. This is the moment "deep learning" goes mainstream.
  </TimelineItem>
  <TimelineItem title="Transformers" date="2017" icon="⚡">
    "Attention Is All You Need" replaces recurrence with self-attention. It parallelizes
    beautifully and becomes the backbone of nearly every modern large model.
  </TimelineItem>
  <TimelineItem title="The LLM era" date="2020s" icon="💬">
    Scaling transformers to billions of parameters yields large language models that
    write, reason, and code — and put ML in the hands of everyday users.
  </TimelineItem>
</Timeline>

## A typical workflow

<Steps>
  <Step title="Frame the problem">
    Decide what you're predicting and what "good" means. Pick a metric *before* you
    start — accuracy, F1, RMSE — so you can't move the goalposts later.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Split the data">
    Carve out **train / validation / test** sets. The test set stays in a vault until
    the very end; touch it early and you'll fool yourself.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Train and tune">
    Fit the model, watch the loss curves, and adjust hyperparameters (learning rate,
    capacity, regularization) against the *validation* set.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Evaluate honestly">
    Report performance on the untouched test set. That number is your real estimate of
    how the model behaves in the wild.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="tip" title="Where to go next">
Pick one supervised problem you care about, fit a small model, and *plot the two loss
curves*. Reading those curves — and reacting to them — is the single most useful habit
a new practitioner can build.
</Callout>
